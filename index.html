But what is this about?

On any given day, users of computers everywhere go through a motley of apps and systems that manage data, transfer it between other computers, and then are able to process and display it in a format that best suits the needs of the user. But under the hood, these seemingly standalone programs often have a clear and traceable history of development that takes inspiration from or directly implements the code of other (past) projects.

It does make one ponder if there is some underlying relation between the people working on these products and to what degree and in what form do these parallels are drawn.

I aim to answer the question: to what extent do the factors of educational level, professional standing, and shared development heritage play a role in how similar the code of software products are.

Throughout this, I will:

  • go over the current status quo of academic studies assessing this similarity and what measure are being employed to quantify and develop on their findings
  • show how the development of free and open source software projects like the operating system unix with its ancestors BSD and Linux present themselves as a superb model for how different software products can be simultaneously created and actively maintained from a ‚Äúsoftware core‚Äù.

Parallel Development? What's that?

In order to ascertain how software products develop in a way that draws from previous software, it is necessary to delve into what parallel development is. In short, it is when 2 or more groups of developers (companies or open source groups) take an existing software product and extend onto it with features specific to what the user base of that group needs (Zhang and Becker, 1).

For example, the codebase of an imaging app could be used by one company to develop a method for enabling the mobile depositing of checks, while another company could be displaying 3D augmented reality models of products that could fit in your home. In both these cases, the apps independently, but simultaneously developed the ability (via code) for the phone to track markers on objects the camera sees such as the signature line on checks, or invisible points on the floor for setting the origin of the AR model.

While the implementation of this tracking ability may be similar, it is worthwhile to note these similarities are of great concern in the case of patents or trademarks on how that process is accomplished, or if there is a bug in the implementation of one of the products and the other product is looking to extend the bug fix for that issue into their own codebase. When these similarities arise, it is often because a lot of the functionality from the base app (the ‚Äúsoftware core‚Äù) like the imaging app exposes its functionality in such a way that it is nigh unavoidable to structure your code in a specific way to handle the control flow and results from their functions.

All of these considerations play a part into why parallel development is an extremely important field to study and analyse.

Parallel Development ‚Æï Variant Analysis

Within the realm of software development, the similarities between the codebases of different products has been thoroughly analysed. It might seem to be exeedingly difficult to visualize how the code of software projects are structured and to what extent similar constructions arise.

However, in academia, this topic has been studied for a decade at this point under the name ‚Äúvariant analysis‚Äù (Duszynski and Becker 2012, 1). Within this landmark study, they look at potential methods in order to:

  • assess the degree to which those commonalities in source code arise such as existing AI models that check for plagiarism in academic assignments
  • detail a novel approach that involves diagramming out the occurrences of certain code sections that perform a trivial operation across different files

In addition to looking into specific code sections, the approach detailed in the paper also addresses another end result of the analysis that obtains a structural similarity, preying on the fact that products in parallel development ‚Äúwere cloned from one another at some point in time‚Äù and as such ‚Äúthey still retain some structural similarity despite later modifications‚Äù (Duszynski and Becker 2012, 3).

This "variant analysis" has exeedingly large utility: they document if other variants contain similar code to what you are working on or if the variants can be combined while still keeping the functionality intact.

This is useful for when one is trying to fix a bug or port over a feature from another program.

Furthermore, this analysis is metricized to the point where the results can be tabulated and plotted on a bar chart, as well as diagrammed via venn diagrams to show just how much code is similar between two variants.

Open source and Unix

It is worthwhile to note that the focus of this study was on the unix operating system and its ancestors, specifically the BSD (Berkeley Software Distribution) and Linux operating systems as they have an easily traceable ancestry with minor changes between the source code, all while being FOSS (free and open source software) allowing for full analysis into its inner workings along with a complete edit history going back to the 80s (‚ÄúLinus Torvalds‚Äù, 2020).

Because of this, it is a prime model of discussion when it comes to software projects and how they evolve. As such, it is worth taking a look at the unix family as a sort of case study to center the goals of this paper around. The Linux project in particular has a large number of people contributing to its development on a monthly basis, encompassing a wide variety of developers at different levels in their career.

The study from Davis and Jabeen in 2011 titled ‚ÄúLearning in the GNU/Linux community‚Äù looked at 4603 people who contributed to the Linux project and conducted a 21 question survey intended to look at the general demographic of the base. They found that when splitting up the age groups into 4 year slices like 18-22 and 33-37, variability in age was not very large as nearly all groups had equal percentages (2, Fig. 2).

This is important because it rules out the notion that older universities and teachings would dominate the ways of thinking and ultimately the design of the source code, at least when it comes to new developments in the project.

Additionally, they looked into the End-User roles (the equivalent of profession standing), and found that 40% of people were in advanced (senior level) roles, with people in beginning (junior level) roles not too far behind at 34% and developing (intern level or interested in the industry) roles much further down at 24% (2, Fig. 4).

Old habits die hard?

Percentages trending downwards in relation to the degree of experience in the work shows that the people working on the Linux project are definitely more exposed to the industry and thus have most likely formed their own best practices or opinions on how certain code is structured.

As such, it is entirely plausible that a certain portion of variant similarity comes from like minded people currently working in enterprise jobs that have been told throughout their career that ‚Äúthis is the best way of doing this task‚Äù and simply talking that dogmatism to heart.

Another side of this is comes from noting that a lot of these developers that fit into the 74% of the senior and junior levels are often contributing as part of or on the side of jobs that they take from development titans such as Intel and Huawei. These companies have their own style guides on how they want code to be structured that tend to be quite similar to one another, increasing stylistic similarity.

Also these companies often have hiring requirements that encourage higher-level education and as such, they would be more exposed to an interpretation of software development that comes from more globalized, generalized, and standardized university curriculums, eventually producing similar code.

Rigged from the start

A lot of the dogmatism and standardization manifest in the works of one of the largest as well as earliest contributors (and also the current maintainer of the Linux project) Linus Torvalds.

Linus has had a tremendous impact on the progress, accessibility, and state of the Linux operating system over the course of nearly 2 decades via aligning with a common practice among software developers at the time, he released the source code, which meant that anyone with knowledge of computer programming could modify Linux to suit their own purposes (‚ÄúLinus Torvalds‚Äù, 2020).

This practice most likely was what led a more recent look into variant analysis in 2017 by Tenev et al to find that the number of lines of code that were similar enough to be considered ‚Äúshared‚Äù between some different distributions of unix was always greater than 50% of the total, while the number of unique lines of code was significantly smaller, never going above 50%.

This demonstrates that while novel code can be written in great quantities, practically, there is a large amount of overlap between the code structure that manifests itself in products that are in common utility.

Where does this lead us?

It does appear that previous research has already assessed just how much of the cross-distribution bug fixes as detailed in the introduction can be done.

In accordance with Dietrich et al, an analysis into the granularity of unix (how certain changes affect the end functionality of unix functions in general) found that as long as the changed code has more to do with the configuration of a program and the way it is built rather than changing internals of the programming languages, most of the changes should be transferable between Linux versions.

This works because most developers would not be tinkering with changes to language implementations, and would instead be tasked with collectivising the functionality of the operating systems into something that works for a specific product of their company.

Additionally, there are more than a thousand distributions of Linux that are able to fit the needs of specific products, like those of mobile and embedded systems all the way to large networking and cloud computing operations; the utility of having these individual solutions be applied to many parallel products is unparalleled (LWN.net).

In the theoretical sense, it is shown that improvements and fixes local to one product can be applied to the litany of Linux distros out in the wild. Hopefully, this resulted in a better understanding of how interconnected the apps of today are and how progressive change results in the ability to create these grand hierarchies of software and its history.
